What is Linear Algebra?

Linear algebra is a branch of mathematics which mainly deals with vector spaces and linear transformations.
These concepts can be represented through vectors and matrices, which are special types of quantities.
The basics of many of these concepts can be explained much more clearly through a geometric intuition.

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -4 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

3-Dimensional Vector

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 2 & -3 \\ 4 & -1 & -8 \\ 9 & 0 & -8 \end{bmatrix} $$

What is a Vector?

In short, vectors are special type of quantity which can't be represented by just one value, but the exact definition of a vector varies slightly from field to field.
For instance, in physics, a vector is anything with a magnitude and a direction, and in other cases, a vector can simply be thought of as a list of numbers.
A generalized definition of a vector however, is anything in which vector addition and scalar multiplication can be meaningfully applied to.
In this section, we'll look over the different ways to represent a vector.

Visual Interpretation

Visually, vectors are usually drawn as an arrow where the length is the magnitude of the vector.
It is important to realize that a vector can have any amount of dimensions. The space we choose to draw the vector in depends on how many dimensions the vector has.
In this example, we will be working with a 2D vector.
To draw the arrow, first determine the direction it will be pointing in. The length of the arrow represents the magnitude.
As long as the direction and magnitude stay the same, this vector can be moved anywhere in space, but generally it is drawn from the origin \((0, 0)\) for readability sakes.

Vector Components

If we draw all of our vectors from the origin, functionally there is no point in drawing the line of the arrow.
This gives us another method of describing a vector using just the arrowhead position.

As you can see, the vector can also be described by the individual x & y coordinates of the arrow tip
These coordinates are called the components of a vector.

Notation

So now that we know the two main methods of representing a vector:

  • Magnitude and Direction
  • Individual Components

How do we actually write and measure these?
There are multiple notations for vectors but the most standard method is using matrix notation with column vectors.

Matrix Notation

This method of specifying a vector places all the individual components of a vector into a list.
If the list is horizontal, then it is a row vector.
If the list is vertical, it is a column vector.
The amount of items in each list is equal to the dimension of the vector.
There's not much difference between both types other than notation. Preferably column vectors are used as they take less horizontal space to write, but in some cases, row vectors will be used in the slides (if it's convenient).
Note: Row vectors and column vectors are not interchangeable within equations.
